Mourners being placed in a holding pen

Mourners are continuing to arrive at the closed queue to see the Queen lying in state - and are now being placed in a holding pen, writes Sky News online reporter Bhvishya Patel.

The official queue to Westminster Hall was closed after hitting capacity at 9.50am, with officials saying it would not be reopened for at least six hours.

Despite this, mourners are still showing up at Southwark Park, where they are being placed in a holding pen.

Before being closed, the queue had reached 4.9 miles long and the waiting time was estimated at 14 hours.

Margaret Wallwork, 76, from Newcastle (above), arrived in London this morning and said she will wait for as long as it takes to pay her respects to the Queen.

"She's been the Queen for as long as I can remember. She's been my Queen and she has served this country very well and she's a lovely lady," she said

"I just want to come down and pay my respects - she's our matriarch.

"She's someone that I really admire."

Paul Banks, 72, from London (below), said: "I only live about five miles away but most of the journey has been walking to join the queue.

"It's an extremely long queue at the moment. 

"I came today because it's a historic event and we should all respect the Queen and the work that she has done for a long time.

"I think it's an event to celebrate her life.

"So many people have been coming out into the streets and everybody is happy. Everybody is well behaved. It's a beautiful day as well - the sun is shining so it's really lovely."

Annie Slater, 40, from Peterborough (below) said: "When I heard (the Queen) had passed away it was like it was a family member.

"When someone in your family dies you go through grief on your own whereas the grief that we're going through now we are experiencing with the nation.

"It's a shared grief which I don't think we'll see again."

Police will only intervene in anti-monarchy protests if 'absolutely necessary'

by Andy Hughes, investigative journalist

Police say they will allow peaceful anti-monarchy protests and will only intervene when "absolutely necessary", as security ramps up for the Queen's funeral. 

The Met says the state funeral on Monday will be the largest police operation ever undertaken in the UK. 

Police chiefs say officers will allow protests but will be "equipped to use their judgement" in dealing with the public. 

It comes after several arrests in Scotland for demonstrating against the Royal Family. 

There have been 34 arrests relating to the Queen’s remembrance activities so far in London for a range of offences – but none have been for protesting. 

Deputy Assistant Commissioner Stuart Cundy said: "We have ensured that all our officers, not just the Met but all colleagues deployed here, understand that people have a right to protest. 

"Our response will be proportionate, it will be balanced and officers will only be taking action where it is absolutely necessary."

DAC Cundy said the force is preparing for a "truly immense operation of huge scale and complexity" on Monday, and cannot be compared to any other event in the history of policing in this country.

Hundreds of world leaders and VIPs will attend the funeral, in addition to the hundreds of thousands of members of the public who have come from all around the UK and the world to pay their respects.

The Met said that the stabbings of two police officers this morning was not believed to be terror related and not related to the Queen's remembrance activities, but has brought a "sharp focus" to officers to remain vigilant. 

DAC Cundy said: "Although we believe this is not related to the Queen's remembrance, and not believed to be terror related, the fact that two colleagues were stabbed brings into sharp focus to keep that vigilance, and for the public to let us know about anything suspicious."

Queen's funeral will be the 'largest single policing event' Scotland Yard has ever tackled

The Metropolitan Police says security for the Queen's funeral will be the largest operation it has ever undertaken.

Large crowds will be watching as prime ministers, presidents and royals gather to pay their respects.  

"I can confirm that this will be the largest single policing event that the Met Police has ever undertaken," Deputy Assistant Commissioner Stuart Cundy told reporters. 

"As a single event, this is larger than the 2012 Olympics. It is larger than the Platinum Jubilee weekend.

"This is a truly immense operation involving a huge amount of specialist officers. 

"It cannot be compared to any other operation in the history of the Met."

Specialist teams include motorbike escort riders, mounted branch, firearms officers, dog teams and the marine unit.

The Met has used more than 22 miles (36km) of barriers in central London alone to control crowds and keep key areas secure.

Mr Cundy added: "Who knows what might occur over the coming days? 

"If anyone sees anything, hears anything, or thinks that something is out of the ordinary please speak to one of the hundreds if not thousands of officers that they'll see so we can respond accordingly."

It was 'just so moving' but 'wear comfortable shoes'

Carriage Gates, in the shadow of Big Ben, is the finishing line of the marathon queue, and as people cross they are physically and emotionally drained, correspondent Ivor Bennett writes.

Some wipe away tears as they emerge from seeing the Queen's coffin, while others take selfies to mark the end of their journey.  

Many embrace the people they've met while lining up.

Natasha Linegar, Jacqueline Crawley, Chris Bullard and Laura Wills joined the queue as strangers at around 8.30pm on Thursday and are now firm friends.

"We're friends for life," said Jacqueline, from Islington in north London.

"We've said we're going to meet again. We've joined a WhatsApp group and we're going to come to the coronation."

It took the group just over nine hours to reach the front of the queue, emerging from Westminster Hall after seeing the Queen's coffin just before 6am.

"It was just so moving. The whole build-up was like a pilgrimage," Natasha, from Liphook in Hampshire, told Sky News.

"The emotion that was there just hits you like a wave."

Chris, from Ipswich, added: "She was such a gracious Queen and she's done so much. To spend the night in the cold to say thanks is the least we can do."

All were dressed warmly for what was a chilly night. 

Their advice to anyone joining the queue now?

"Wear comfortable shoes", said Laura, from Dorset.

"There's a lot of standing around."

The Queen's eight grandchildren will hold a 15-minute vigil around the Queen's coffin on Saturday evening.

The Prince of Wales will stand at the head of the coffin, with the Duke of Sussex at the foot.

Queen on 'very good form' last year - Welsh first minister

Welsh First Minister Mark Drakeford has been sharing an "abiding memory" of the Queen.

Asked to recall an encounter with Her Majesty, Mr Drakeford told Sky News: "Well, if I close my eyes and think of a memory, it would be of less than a year ago when the Queen came to Cardiff to open the latest term of the Welsh parliament. 

"The Queen had come to Wales to open every single session of the assembly. And in October last year, at a time when we knew she was beginning to step back from her more strenuous duties, she came to Cardiff."

The first minister added: "She was on very good form, very alert, very engaged, very keen to talk to all the people who were there wishing to talk to her. 

"And I think that will be an abiding memory for me."

The Queen at the Welsh parliament in October 2021PA

Mr Drakeford said he'd had an "opportunity of a telephone conversation with the New Prince of Wales".

Prince William said he "wanted to take on his new responsibilities slowly", the first minister confided.

Mr Drakeford continued: "And I think that was a very wise approach that he wants to give himself time to become familiar with conditions of contemporary Wales, to think of those causes that he will be able to associate himself with in order to make a success of them. 

"He knows North Wales, particularly, of course, because he lived there while he worked as an emergency helicopter pilot working out of Anglesey. 

"And I think that he is looking forward to deepening that relationship, and to give himself the time he will need to do so."

Three central London Tube stations to be closed on Monday morning

Three central London Tube stations will be closed for most of Monday morning to avoid overcrowding.

Passengers will not be able to start or end journeys at Westminster, St James's Park and Hyde Park Corner, Transport for London (TfL) said.

It will "aim to reopen stations" after the funeral to help people get home from the Westminster area.

Green Park station will be exit-only between 10am and 8pm.

In addition, many buses in central London will be diverted because of road closures.

Buses will pull over "if it is safe and practical to do so" and switch their engines off during the one-minute silence on Sunday at 8pm and the two-minute silence at around 11.55am on Monday, TfL said.
