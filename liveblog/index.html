Boris Johnson to unveil his COVID winter plan

An ambitious winter plan to fight COVID-19 - including booster jabs and flu vaccinations for millions - is being unveiled by Boris Johnson, writes Sky News chief political correspondent Jon Craig.

The Prime Minister is promising a bonfire of lockdown restrictions, but will keep the threat of compulsory face masks and vaccine passports in reserve.

And other restrictions such as compulsory home working and social distancing are not being ruled out if there is a sudden jump in COVID cases.

In a Downing Street address, Mr Johnson will declare that he is firmly opposed to another lockdown and suggest that the UK must learn to live with COVID now all adults have been offered a vaccine.

Parental consent will be sought but there will be instances where teenagers can override parents - Zahawi

Providing more details on how the vaccination programme will work for 12 to 15-year-olds, the vaccines minister says the jabs will be administered in schools. 

Parental consent will be sought and "handled by each school in the usual way", he says, with children provided with a leaflet about the vaccine. 

In the "rare event" where a parent does not consent but the teenager wants to have the vaccine, there will be a process implemented by schools.

Labour welcomes decision and says children have been 'among biggest victims' of pandemic

Responding to Mr Zahawi's statement, shadow health secretary Jonathan Ashworth says Labour is pleased with the decision. 

"Children may not have been the face of this crisis but they have been amongst its biggest victims," he says. 

He commends the scientists for understanding the "importance of avoiding the disruption of being out of school", adding we still don't know the mental health impacts of so much time spent out of classrooms. 

However, he says many constituents will "rightly have questions", including when the vaccines will be rolled out and how vaccination rates can be improved among adults.

Jabs will be offered to 12 to 15-year-olds, vaccines minister confirms

Speaking in the Commons, the vaccines minister says the programme will be rolled out with "the same urgency" as that used to vaccinate adults. 

However, he adds it is important that parents and children are not "stigmatised" for the decision they come to. 

"As a father, the decisions I take on the behalf of my own children give me extra pause for thought," he says, accepting that parents "naturally have more questions when it comes to vaccinating their children". 

But he offers them some reassurance the vaccines are safe, telling the Commons that the UK's medicines regulator is "the best in the world" and has "rigorously" assessed the safety of the Pfizer vaccine for this age group.

Analysis: Why the vaccination programme for children may have been undermined
By Ashish Joshi, health correspondent

In the space of a fortnight, the government has had two recommendations about vaccinating 12 to 15-year-olds. 

The first one was from the JCVI, who said it was not necessary. The government scientists said inoculating this age group would only offer marginal health benefits. 

They then advised the government to seek further clarification and guidance from the four chief medical officers of the UK - who looked at the evidence and said it is in the best interest of all children aged 12-15 to be vaccinated against COVID 19. 

Recognising the potential for sending mixed messages, Professor Chris Whitty explained why there was no conflict.

New York children return to school

Around a million children returned to classrooms in New York city today, as Mayor Bill de Blasio seeks to get the metropolis back up and running.

Most of the city's 300,000 public employees were also expected to return to their workplace, as remote working and learning is rolled back.

Source: AP

It is the largest return to in-person teaching in the US so far.

Teachers are required to be vaccinated, while city workers can be vaccinated or take weekly tests to keep their jobs.

While some places in the US are offering remote learning, Mr de Blasio has ruled it out.

He said: "There are kids who have not been in a classroom in a year and a half, and they deserve better.

"Kids need to be back in school for their mental health, their physical health, their ability to develop socially, and for so many reasons."
