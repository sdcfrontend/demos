Analysis: How the little moves today are adding up to a bigger picture

By Nick Martin, people and politics correspondent 

There's been a fair share of surprises today. 

If we cast our minds back to yesterday, it was certainly a dramatic day. Cabinet reshuffles tell us a lot about the course that the prime minister wants to set the government on. 

So what we're starting to see today as the bigger picture is a clear-out. 

Firstly, the pattern that emerged next door to the prime minister in the Treasury was lots of junior ministers aligned to Chancellor Rishi Sunak already gone. 

They've been replaced by those who you could argue are loyal to Boris Johnson. 

And then with Michael Gove going into housing, there are rumours tonight it might not be called housing any more. It might actually become a department for "levelling up". 

The government is gazing towards the general election now, with promises to level up huge swathes of the country - not just Red Wall areas but parts of Tory heartland.

Dutch foreign affairs minister resigns over handling of Afghanistan evacuation

Dominic Raab is not the only politician to have faced the consequences of the coalition's handling of the Afghanistan crisis. 

Dutch foreign affairs minister Sigrid Kaag resigned after the lower house of parliament passed a motion of censure against the government over its handling of evacuations. 

Ms Kaag acknowledged her government's failings had meant some local staff members and translators had not managed to be evacuated. 

Dutch foreign affairs minister Sigrid Kaag

After the motion of censure was passed, she said: "I can only accept the consequences of this judgment as the minister with ultimate responsibility." 

In contrast, British former foreign secretary Dominic Raab faced calls for his resignation for days - and later told the Foreign Affairs Committee he had never considered stepping down. 

Instead, he was demoted to justice secretary during Boris Johnson's cabinet reshuffle yesterday.

Reshuffle round-up: What has happened so far today?

After rearranging his cabinet yesterday, Boris Johnson has today been reshuffling the more junior ranks of his government.

Penny Mordaunt has been moved to the Department for International Trade after being replaced as Paymaster General in the Cabinet Office by former solicitor general Michael Ellis.

Meanwhile, John Whittingdale has been removed from the Department for Digital, Culture, Media and Sport.

It means there might still be a few appointments to be announced by Downing Street this evening, with gaps still missing in some ministerial roles.

There is yet to be news on who will replace Mr Ellis as solicitor general, while it has yet to be confirmed if Nadhim Zahawi will be replaced as COVID vaccines minister after his promotion to education secretary.

And we have yet to hear who will be replacing Nick Gibb after he was removed as schools minister.

16 Sept 19:13

PM shows his political cunning by elevating potential heirs

Analysis by Sam Coates, deputy political editor

One of the most fascinating aspects of this reshuffle was how it set up possible tensions that could come to define this government in the years ahead.

Central to this is Boris Johnson himself, whose choices demonstrate the eye of a political tactician. 

Before the reshuffle he had one heir apparent in a great office of state, now he has two.

By making Liz Truss foreign secretary, and having Rishi Sunak continue on at the Treasury, he has ensured he has two possible heirs in high-profile roles. 

That's an interesting decision to take, some might say quite cunning - he wants them competing with each other rather than directly with him.

Dominic Raab's move was planned, No 10 spokesman says

Mr Raab was moved from foreign secretary to deputy prime minister in yesterday's reshuffle, prompting speculation he was given the new role to appease him after taking away one of the top jobs in the cabinet.

Boris Johnson's spokesman today said it was "a planned move".

He added that becoming deputy prime minister "formalises" his position as deputy. Mr Raab stepped in for the PM when he was in hospital with COVID last year.

The title also demonstrates his seniority in government and he will stand in for the PM at Prime Minister's Questions when needed (most likely next week), the spokesman added.

16 Sept 19:07

John Whittingdale's ousting is important as he was leading the controversial privatisation of Channel 4

Analysis by Joe Pike, political correspondent

Among the most significant of today's middle-ranking ministerial sackings is the departure of John Whittingdale, first reported by Sky News. 

That is because he has been spearheading the controversial privatisation of Channel 4, and some believe a new minister could shift the government's approach.

A veteran political operator, Mr Whittingdale advised Margaret Thatcher in the 1980s. 

He was culture secretary under David Cameron and took a more junior job in the same department under Boris Johnson. He is said to be close to the PM's wife, Carrie Johnson, his former special adviser.

Some in the media industry believe that without the "privatisation-evangelist" Mr Whittingdale in post, the whole Channel 4 conundrum may be kicked into the long grass. 

Others within government point out that it would never be the media minister making this huge decision, or even the secretary of state, but the prime minister. And Boris Johnson does not need to change personnel solely so he can change his mind.

Oliver Dowden, the culture secretary until yesterday, was due to give a speech that evening to the high-profile Royal Television Society media conference.
