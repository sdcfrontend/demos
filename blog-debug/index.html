R number in England rises slightly to 0.8 to 1.1

Last week, the R value â or reproduction number â was 0.8 to 1.0.

The R number indicates the average number of people each COVID-positive person goes on to infect.

The latest growth rate range for England is -3% to +1%, according to the UK Health Security Agency (UHSA).

This means the number of infections could be broadly flat, shrinking by up to 3% every day, or growing by up to 1% every day.

The latest government figures show the R number is between 0.8 to 1.1 in each region of England except for London, where it is estimated to be between 0.8 to 1.0.

They also show the growth rate varies across regions, with the East of England and the Midlands both currently having one of -3% to +2%.

London and the South West are both at -3% to +1%, in the North East and Yorkshire it is -2% to +2%, the North West -2% to +1%, while in the South East it is -4% to +1%.

Merck says antiviral COVID pill cuts deaths in half

The pharmaceutical company said the drug - molnupiravir - reduced COVID hospitalisations and deaths by 50% in people recently infected with the virus. 

If cleared, Merck's pill would be the first shown to treat COVID, a potentially major advance in efforts to fight the pandemic. 

The study results were released by the company and have not yet been peer reviewed. 

However, an independent group of medical advisers monitoring the trial recommended stopping it early because the interim results were so strong. 

Merck and its partner Ridgeback Biotherapeutics said early results showed patients who received the drug within five days of displaying COVID symptoms had half the rate of hospitalisation and death as those who received a placebo.

The study tracked 775 adults with mild-to-moderate COVID who were considered higher risk for severe disease due to health problems such as obesity, diabetes or heart disease.

Possible link between rare blood clots and COVID jab â EMA

There is a possible link between rare cases of blood clots and Johnson & Johnson's COVID vaccine, according to the European Medicines Agency. 

The EU's drug regulator has recommended that the condition is listed as a side-effect of the jab.

It also recommended that immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) be added as an adverse reaction with unknown frequency to the J&J vaccine product information and to AstraZeneca's jab. 

This is a bleeding disorder caused by the body mistakenly attacking platelets.

Russia hits record COVID deaths for fourth day in a row

Russia on Friday recorded its highest daily death toll for the fourth consecutive day, with 887 fatalities confirmed.

COVID rates rise for school aged children - ONS

The Office for National Statistics estimated that COVID rates have increased for children aged between two and 16-years-old.

There were also early signs of a possible increase for people aged 70 and over, ONS said.

The percentage testing positive decreased for those in school Year 12 to age 24, and levelled off for people aged 35 to 69. 

Around one in 20 children in school Years 7 to 11 are estimated to have had COVID in the week to 25 September - the highest positivity rate for any age group.

Elsewhere, the ONS found that Yorkshire and the Humber had the highest COVID infection rate, at one in 55 people.

London had the lowest at around one in 130.

One in 85 people in England had COVID last week - ONS

The latest data shows around one in 85 people in private households in England tested positive for COVID in the week ending 25 September.

This is up from one in 90 in the previous week, according to the Office for National Statistics.

One in 85 is the equivalent of about 658,800 people.

This compares to one in 55 people in Wales and Scotland, and one in 65 people in Northern Ireland.

Both Scotland and Northern Ireland saw a reduction in estimated infection rates, whereas Wales noted a rise.

At least 50 countries miss 10% vaccine target

The majority of countries that missed the World Health Organisation's vaccination target are in Africa, the BBC reports.

The goal to vaccinate 10% of every country's population by 30 September was set in May by the World Health Assembly.

Almost 90% of high-income nations have met this target, with the UK having fully vaccinated nearly 66% of its population and the US trailing behind at 55%.

Those to miss the mark include war-torn nations such as Yemen, Syria and Afghanistan, and those like Haiti which have been hit by natural disasters.

Additionally, half of the 52 African countries that have received COVID jabs have fully vaccinated just 2% or less of their populations.
