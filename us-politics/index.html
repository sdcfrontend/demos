Divided
States

    By: Christopher Scott

    •

    The United States is now more divided than it has been for decades, fractured into two distinct camps: Democrats and Republicans.

    It is a nation so polarised that eight out of 10 Americans say that they disagree with the other party not only on policy but basic facts. The most divisive issues are the two parties’ differing views on President Donald Trump, gun control, abortion and immigration.

    Trump - the most divisive president of a generation – is fighting midterm elections in 2018 and a series of scandals which could seriously impact his time in the White House. If the Democrats win enough seats in the midterms and full control of Congress they gain the power to remove the president from office.

    Donald Trump

    No one personifies the partisan divide quite like President Trump. Among Republicans, Trump has an approval rating of 88%, but among Democrats that figure is just 8%.

    Trump's supporters perceive him as the strongman the country needs; tough on borders, crime and good for the economy. Most Democrats, however, consider Trump unfit for office.

    The Trump administration has found itself embroiled in numerous scandals in the past two years, not least the ongoing investigation into whether Russia helped Trump win the presidency.

    Trump and his supporters believe the investigation is a witch-hunt. Some Trump supporters have even taken to wearing shirts at rallies that read "I'd rather be a Russian than a Democrat", something unthinkable only a few years ago.

    Midterms Elections

    The midterm elections – held every two years after a presidential election – are used to elect senators and congressmen.

    In 2016, Republicans won control of Congress. In the House of Representatives, Republicans hold 236 seats to the Democrats' 193. In the Senate, Republicans hold a slim majority of 50 seats to 47.

    In 2018, 35 Senate seats are on the ballot, as are all 435 House seats. Therefore, the Democrats will need to gain 25 seats in the midterms to reclaim the House, while they only need to gain two seats to flip the Senate.

    Republicans have cause to worry too, history tells us the president's party averages losses of 30 seats in Congress during midterm elections. In fact, it is only in 1934, during the Great Depression, and in 2002, a year after 9/11, that the president's party has actually gained seats in both the House and Senate

    Abortion

    The issue of abortion rights has defined American politics for the past half century and is the most divisive subject in the US. But, that wasn't always the case.

    In 1973, the Supreme Court ruled in Roe v Wade - one of the most well-known American court cases of all time - that abortion was a fundamental right under the US Constitution. At that time though the law passed with little controversy in a 7-2 ruling.

    Abortion was seen by the majority of Americans as a women's health issue because illegal and botched abortions had resulted in deaths. But after the ruling, abortion became a lightning rod for the evangelical Christian movement which claimed that abortion was murder and an immoral act.

    Attitudes to abortion Far more Republicans believe abortion should be illegal than Democrats.

    Ever since Roe v Wade, the country has been split, again, down party lines. The majority of Democrats align themselves with the pro-choice movement – a women's right to govern her own body – while most Republicans see themselves as pro-life – that life starts at conception.

    Many Republican voters have been trying to overturn Roe v Wade ever since it passed, electing pro-lifers to office who would then appoint pro-life judges to local and state courts and most importantly the Supreme Court.

    In 1992, Roe v Wade faced its biggest challenge to date with the Supreme Court case of Planned Parenthood v Casey. Republicans thought they would finally overturn the law as the court had eight justices appointed by Republican presidents.

    However, Roe v Wade was upheld in a narrow 5-4 ruling, with Justice Anthony Kennedy casting the deciding vote.

    But with Kennedy retiring from the Court in the summer of 2018, Roe v Wade is under threat yet again as President Trump has said he would only appoint pro-life judges to the Court.

    At the moment the Court is split 4-4 and abortion rights are once again in the balance.

    Immigration

    President Trump's immigration policies have arguably been his most controversial.

    He has proposed a travel ban on Muslims from entering the US, called Mexicans crossing the border "rapists and murderers" and promised to build a wall to keep them out.

    His administration has also come under fire for separating families who have illegally come to the United States and locking undocumented children in cages.
