State of the Nations

By David Mercer and Carmen Aguilar Garcia

  •

The vast majority of Britons believe the country is divided and fear for the future of the UK economy amid the political turmoil over Brexit, a Sky Data poll has found.

This wide-ranging survey has shed light on the mood of the nation as the UK prepares to leave the European Union, revealing the level of distrust felt towards institutions - from the government to the mainstream media.

In the most troubling findings, more than half of the country believe the UK public is racist and Islamophobic, while nearly nine in 10 people think political leaders do not care about the public.

But surprisingly, given the uncertainty facing the country, most Britons say they are happy in their lives and have a more positive outlook about their personal futures than the UK’s as a whole.

Here, Sky News delves into the results to reveal the state of the nation.

Divided UK

Three quarters (75%) of Britons believe the UK is divided - with the percentage even higher for the country’s younger generation.

But why do the majority of the British public believe society is divided?

More than four in 10 Britons (42%) said the main factor was Brexit, while 23% blamed politicians and 14% said immigration was the main cause.

Rob Johns, professor of politics at the University of Essex, said the British public often linked questions about Europe with their views on Brexit.

“We have long talked very lazily about being in Europe as a political decision as opposed to a geographical fact,” he said.

“It is a noticeable the proportion of people who say they have some European identity has gone up. I think that’s because Remainers have learnt to say they’re European.”

The poll also revealed a higher trust in institutions like the Royal Family, the police and the armed forces, compared to the government, political parties and media.

Nearly half (47%) said they distrusted the government in the wake of Brexit, compared to 45% who distrust the Tory party, 40% who distrust Labour and 30% who distrust the mainstream media.

The government and the two main political parties have some of the lowest scores for confidence, but also are the most affected regarding the perception of what they have said and done over Brexit.

Many Britons feel betrayed by the country’s institutions over the process of Britain leaving the EU, from the government and political parties to the media and big business.

Dissatisfaction with politics go beyond the institutions. According to the poll, a whopping 87% of Britons felt political leaders did not care about them, although a third admitted they had little understanding of events in British politics.

Political dissatisfaction

Youngest generation are the most dissatisfied with politicians, with 90% of 18 to 24-year-olds thinking political leaders don’t care about them.

90%

Prof Johns said the proportion of people who felt political leaders did not care about them showed a “very decided public mood”.

“This is much less a comment on politicians in general and much more a comment on how a polarised electorate is viewing how politicians are handling Brexit,” he said.

Half (51%) said their lives were going in a good direction, but just 21% felt the same way about the UK economy and one in five (20%) about the UK overall.

And an even higher proportion of Britons believe the UK public is Islamophobic.

Fiyaz Mugal, founder of Tell Mama, an organisation which monitors anti-Muslim hate crime, said Islamophobia was growing in the UK but the perception of the level appeared to be greater than the reality.

“There was significant levels of racism in Britain in the late 1970s and ‘80s and many of us suffered open ‘in your face’ violence,” he said.

“The younger population don’t have that experience of what happened before - but they do come across comments and information online that is racist and Islamophobic.

“There is a problem of growing Islamophobia… but I think the perception may be more significant than the reality. Incidents get flared up quickly on social media.

“While I think the perception may be more than the reality, it doesn’t take away that there is a growing cancer in our society of bigotry and prejudice.”

Personal happiness

Despite the political turmoil over Brexit, the Sky Data poll revealed the vast majority of Britons are happy with their lives.

Seven in 10 (70%) said they were happy in life in general, compared to 17% who said they were unhappy.

Over-75s were the happiest age group (77%), compared to 65% of 18 to 24-year-olds said they were happy.

British people were most happy about their family and friends (88%) followed by their housing situation (78%) and health (67%).

Michael Plant, an Oxford University philosopher who researches happiness, said: “The evidence shows that the biggest contributors to unhappiness are poor mental health, unemployment and loneliness. 

“People often don’t consider politics when answering questions about how happy they are because - quite correctly - they see it doesn’t have that much impact on their daily lives.  

“Social media can boost happiness if we use it to connect with one another. However it can have the opposite effect when we spend time enviously viewing other people’s lives.”

Despite the fact that family and friends are the main reason for happiness, seven in 10 Britons said they have only three or fewer people they could depend on to help them if they were in a serious financial difficulties.

People felt their social circle increases when it comes to dealing with a health problem.

About half (53%) said they had three friends or more to rely on if they had a serious physical health problem, while the percentage was 50% for a serious mental health problem.
